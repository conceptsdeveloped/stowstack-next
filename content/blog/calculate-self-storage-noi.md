Ask most operators what their facility is worth and you get a guess. Ask them their NOI and you get a longer pause.

That's backwards. NOI is the number your facility is priced on. A buyer, a lender, a broker, the bank that holds your loan, they all start in the same place: net operating income. If you don't know yours to the dollar, you're flying a plane without an altimeter.

The good news is the math is not hard. You don't need an accountant or a spreadsheet you built three years ago and stopped trusting. You need an hour with your bank statements and the right list of line items.

## What NOI actually is

NOI is what the property earns after the cost of running it, before financing and taxes.

**NOI = effective gross income − operating expenses**

That's the whole formula. The work is in the two halves.

**Effective gross income (EGI)** is what you actually collect. Start with gross potential rent, which is every unit full at your street rate. Knock off vacancy, concessions, and the tenants who skip out owing you money. Then add the income that isn't rent: tenant insurance commissions, late and lien fees, admin fees at move-in, the locks and boxes you sell at the counter, truck rental commissions, a cell tower lease if you've got one.

**Operating expenses** are what it costs to keep the gate open. For self-storage that's a specific list, and it's shorter than people think.

## The storage expense list, line by line

Here's what belongs in your operating expenses. Pull each one off your bank statements for the trailing twelve months:

- **Payroll** for your manager and any relief or part-time help, plus the employer side of payroll taxes and benefits
- **Property taxes** on the parcel
- **Property and liability insurance** (this is your building coverage, not the tenant protection plan)
- **Utilities** for lights, the gate, and climate control
- **Repairs and maintenance** for doors, springs, the gate motor, asphalt patching, HVAC service
- **Marketing and advertising**
- **Office and admin**, including your management software, phone, and internet
- **Credit card and merchant fees** on the rent you collect
- **Security and gate monitoring**
- **Grounds**: landscaping, snow removal, pest control
- **Professional fees** for legal and accounting
- **A property management fee** if a third party runs the place, usually 5 to 6 percent of what they collect

Add those up. Subtract from EGI. That's your NOI.

> **Operator Note:** The number that trips people up is the management fee. Even if you self-manage, a buyer will underwrite one in, because they're paying someone to run it. If you're going to sell, run your NOI with a market management fee in it so you're looking at the same number the buyer is.

## What to leave out (this is where people blow it)

Four things do not belong in NOI, and folding them in is the most common mistake I see:

1. **Your mortgage.** Debt service sits below NOI. It's how you financed the deal, not how the property performs.
2. **Capital expenditures.** A new roof, repaving the lot, replacing a run of doors. Those are capital, not operating. Across the industry, CapEx runs about 8 percent of NOI for storage, lower than most other commercial real estate, but it's still its own line, not an operating cost.
3. **Depreciation and amortization.** Paper expenses. They don't touch the cash the property throws off.
4. **Income taxes.** Yours, not the property's.

Why be strict about this? Because NOI is only useful if it's comparable. The whole point is that your facility and the one down the road can be measured the same way, regardless of who financed what or how the accountant booked depreciation. Bury your loan payment in there and you've understated your NOI and quietly cut what your facility is worth.

## Why NOI is the number that sets your value

Storage trades on a cap rate. Value is just NOI divided by that rate.

**Value = NOI ÷ cap rate**

Cap rates for storage have been sitting around 5.8 percent. Run the math at that rate and every extra $10,000 of annual NOI adds roughly $172,000 of value. Every ten grand. That's the upside hiding in the NOI line.

And NOI in this asset class has been a grinder. Average annual NOI growth since 2008 has been 4.4 percent, beating inflation, and storage has posted positive NOI growth in every major recession since 2000. It's a $432 billion asset class that keeps earning when other real estate stalls. The number is worth getting right.

> **Key Takeaway:** NOI isn't an accounting chore. It's the dial your facility's value turns on. Move the dial and the value moves with it, at roughly 17 to 1 at today's cap rates.

## The fastest way to move it

Here's the part most operators skip. Two levers move NOI faster than anything else, and you control both.

**Fill more units.** A vacant unit earns nothing and costs you almost the same as a full one. Occupancy flows almost straight to NOI. This is why marketing is a value-creation lever, not a cost. Fill ten more 10x10s at $95 and you've added more than $11,000 of annual NOI, worth nearly $200,000 at a 5.8 percent cap. That's what a full gate is actually worth.

**Hold and raise rate.** An existing-customer rate increase has no acquisition cost and lands almost entirely in NOI. The risk is move-outs, which is exactly why you want enough new move-ins coming in to replace anyone who leaves.

That's the whole game. Fill units, hold rate, watch NOI climb, watch value climb with it. The REITs run this playbook every quarter. Nothing stops you from running it too.
